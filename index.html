Section 1 — The National Picture

Forty years of building approvals data reveal a market shaped by economic cycles, policy interventions, and structural shifts toward higher-density living. Key events — the GFC, the mining boom, COVID stimulus, and the rate-hike cycle — each left visible marks on approval volumes.

Are We Building Enough?

Australia needs approximately 240,000 new dwellings per year to house its growing population — a target set by the National Housing Accord. The rolling 12-month total has rarely reached this threshold, and currently sits well below it.

The 2021 stimulus boom briefly brought approvals close to the target, but the 13 consecutive interest rate rises from May 2022 caused a sharp fall — particularly in apartment approvals, which are highly sensitive to financing costs.

At the current pace, Australia will fall approximately 80,000–100,000 dwellings short of the annual target, deepening the supply deficit.

Section 2 — Where Is Australia Building?

Building Activity by State

Victoria leads the nation in raw approval volumes (56,581 in FY2024–25), followed by New South Wales (49,515) and Queensland (38,229). Together, these three states account for over 75% of all Australian building approvals.

However, raw totals reflect population size. On a per-capita basis, the rankings shift considerably — with Western Australia and Melbourne showing the strongest supply responses relative to their populations.

The ACT's dramatic collapse (−37.8% YoY) stands out as the most severe decline, driven by the near-halt of apartment development in Canberra amid high interest rates and developer caution.

The bubble map shows each capital city's building activity for FY2024–25. Bubble size represents total approvals; colour shows the approvals-per-1,000-residents rate. Melbourne's dominance is clear in size, but Adelaide and Perth punch above their weight in per-capita supply.

Western Australia (+31.4%) is the standout performer — strong migration inflows have driven a construction boom. Queensland and NSW also show solid growth. The ACT (−37.8%) and South Australia (−5.2%) are the only jurisdictions recording declines.

Section 3 — What Type of Homes Are We Building?

Perth (79.6% houses) and Darwin (82.4%) are land-abundant cities favouring detached housing. Canberra (ACT) has the highest apartment share at 75.1% — reflecting policy-driven densification. Sydney sits at 61.3% apartments, reflecting its constrained land market and high house prices driving demand for units.

Section 4 — Is Supply Meeting Demand?

Population Growth vs Supply Rate

This scatter plot joins two ABS data sources: building approvals (8731.0) and regional population (3218.0). Each bubble is a state or territory; size represents population.

States in the top-right quadrant have both high population growth and strong supply — Western Australia is the clear leader. States in the bottom-right are growing fast but not building fast enough.

The connected dot plot below shows each capital city's building supply rate (approvals per 1,000 residents) alongside its population demand pressure (annual growth %). Cities where the orange dot sits far right of the blue dot are under the most acute supply stress.

Melbourne shows the best supply-demand balance. Sydney has high demand but weak supply — only 5.95 approvals per 1,000 residents despite 1.35% annual growth. Darwin's very low supply rate (2.29) relative to its growth (1.74%) is also concerning.

Section 5 — The Long View

Hover over a city line to highlight it. Melbourne is on track to overtake Sydney as Australia's largest city within the decade.

Growth is overwhelmingly concentrated in outer-suburban greenfield LGAs — Melton, Wyndham, and Cardinia in Victoria; Ipswich and Moreton Bay in Queensland. These are the areas where housing demand is most acute and infrastructure is most stretched.
