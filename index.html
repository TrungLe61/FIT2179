Section 1

The National Picture

Forty years of building approvals reveal a volatile history shaped by economic crises, government stimulus, and population booms. The underlying question: are we building enough?

National Building Approvals, 1983–2026
Monthly approvals for houses and non-house dwellings (apartments, units, townhouses). Annotated with major economic events.
The COVID-era stimulus spike (2020–21) drove approvals to near-record highs, but rapid interest rate hikes from May 2022 caused a sharp contraction — particularly in apartment approvals.
Are We Building Enough? Rolling 12-Month Total
Australia needs approximately 200,000 new dwellings per year to meet population demand. The dashed red line marks that target.
Australia has only briefly exceeded 200,000 annual approvals during the 2021 stimulus boom. In the current rate-hike environment, we are running roughly 25,000 dwellings short each year.
Section 2

Where Is Australia Building?

Building activity is heavily concentrated in Victoria, New South Wales, and Queensland — but on a per-capita basis, the picture changes dramatically. Western Australia is leading the recovery while the ACT has collapsed.

Building Approvals by State — FY 2024–25
Total dwelling approvals by state and territory. Hover to explore values.
Capital City Approvals — Bubble Map
Bubble size = total FY2024–25 approvals. Colour = approvals per 1,000 residents.
State Year-on-Year Change in Approvals (FY23–24 vs FY24–25)
Which states are accelerating construction and which are falling behind?
Western Australia (+31.4%) and Queensland (+12.4%) are the standout performers, driven by strong migration. The ACT's −37.8% collapse reflects high interest rates crushing apartment development in Canberra.
Section 3

What Type of Homes Are We Building?

Australia's housing mix has shifted dramatically over four decades, with apartments and units taking a larger share. But this shift is far from uniform across cities.

Houses vs Apartments — National Trend (1983–2026)
Stacked monthly approvals showing the long-run shift in dwelling composition.
Non-house dwellings (apartments, units, townhouses) have grown from under 20% of approvals in the 1980s to routinely exceeding 40% today — reflecting densification in major cities.
House vs Apartment Mix by Capital City — FY 2024–25
Each city has a dramatically different dwelling mix. Perth and Darwin build mostly houses; Sydney and Canberra skew heavily towards apartments.
Perth (79.6% houses) and Darwin reflect land-abundant cities favouring detached housing. Sydney (38.7% houses) and Canberra (24.9%) show high-density urban markets constrained by land costs.
Section 4

Is Supply Meeting Demand?

Raw approval counts don't tell the full story. When adjusted for population size and growth, the supply-demand mismatch reveals which cities face the most acute housing pressure.

Approvals per 1,000 Residents by State
States above the national average are better keeping pace with population. Colour shows above/below average.
Population Growth vs Supply Rate (States)
X = annual population growth rate; Y = approvals per 1,000 residents. Bubble size = population. States in the top-right quadrant have both high demand and strong supply response.
Supply vs Demand Race by Capital City
Each city shows two dots: supply rate (approvals per 1,000 people, normalised 0–10) and demand pressure (population growth %, normalised 0–10). Cities where the orange dot is far right of the blue dot face acute supply shortfalls.
Melbourne and Adelaide show the strongest supply response relative to demand. Sydney and Darwin show the opposite — high demand pressure with comparatively weak supply. Perth is surging on both metrics.
Section 5

The Long View

Population growth trajectories show where demand will be concentrated over the coming decade — and which local government areas are already straining under growth pressure.

Capital City Population Growth, 2001–2025
Each line represents a capital city GCCSA. Melbourne and Sydney have added over 1.5 million residents each since 2001.
Melbourne is on track to surpass Sydney as Australia's largest city within a decade. Perth, Brisbane, and Adelaide are all accelerating, while Darwin and Hobart remain far smaller.
Top 20 Fastest-Growing LGAs in Australia (FY 2024–25)
Local Government Areas ranked by population growth rate. Colour indicates state. These are the areas where housing demand is most acute.
Growth hotspots are heavily concentrated in outer-suburban greenfield areas (Melton, Wyndham, Cardinia in VIC; Ipswich, Moreton Bay in QLD) — where land is cheaper but infrastructure is stretched.
