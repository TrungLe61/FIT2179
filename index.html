Why Australia Isn't Building Enough Homes

Australia isn't building enough new homes to keep up with its growing population. Since 1983, the number of homes given the green light to be built has gone up and down sharply, driven by rising interest rates, financial crises, and government decisions. The government's goal is to approve at least 240,000 new homes every year, but this target is almost never reached. This visualisation explores where homes are being built, what kinds, and whether supply is keeping up with demand.

Part 1

What's Happening Across the Country

Forty years of data tell a story shaped by big events. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis (a worldwide economic crash) caused a sudden drop in new home approvals. A later resources boom gave a brief boost. During COVID in 2020–21, a government building stimulus temporarily pushed numbers up. Then from 2022, the Reserve Bank raised interest rates 13 times in a row, making home loans more expensive, resulting in approvals fell to their lowest level in decades.

Are Enough New Homes Being Built?

The government has set a goal of approving at least 240,000 new homes every year to house Australia's growing population. But in reality, this target is almost never hit. Right now, only around 160,000 homes are being approved per year, well short of what the nation currently needs.

A government scheme in 2021 briefly pushed numbers close to the target. But from May 2022, the Reserve Bank raised interest rates 13 times in a row, making it much more expensive to borrow loan to build. This hit apartment construction hard. At the current rate, Australia is on track to be short by around 80,000 homes compared to what the target requires.

Part 2

Which States Are Building the Most?

Explore Building Activity by State

Click on a state to zoom in and see more detail. Each region on the map is shaded based on how quickly its population is growing. The dot over the capital city shows how many new homes were approved for every 1,000 people living there.

Victoria approved the most new homes this year (56,581), followed by New South Wales (49,515) and Queensland (38,229). Hover your mouse over any area or city dot to see the full numbers.

While Victoria and NSW approve the most dwellings in raw terms, the per-capita picture tells a different story. Melbourne leads on rate (8.56 per 1,000), closely followed by Adelaide (7.94) and Perth (7.93). Darwin approves the fewest relative to its population, reflecting limited construction activity in the Territory.

Where Is Growth Accelerating?

Western Australia (+31.4%) is the standout performer, driven by strong migration inflows. The ACT (−37.8%) and South Australia (−5.2%) are the only jurisdictions recording declines. Blue = growth; red = decline.

The ACT's dramatic collapse (−37.8% YoY) stands out as the most severe decline, driven by the near-halt of apartment development in Canberra amid high interest rates.
Section 3

What Type of Homes Are We Building?

The long-term shift from detached houses to apartments is one of the defining structural trends in Australian housing. In 1983, houses dominated; today apartments account for roughly 40% of all approvals nationally — and far more in Sydney and Melbourne.

A Tale of Two Cities

Perth (79.6% houses) and Darwin (82.4%) are land-abundant cities where detached housing dominates. Canberra has the highest apartment share at 75.1% — reflecting deliberate densification policy.

Sydney sits at 61.3% apartments, driven by its constrained land market and high house prices pushing demand toward units. The housing mix matters for affordability: high apartment shares can indicate either a well-functioning density market or a failure to deliver affordable detached homes.

Section 4

Is Supply Meeting Demand?

Raw approval totals favour large states by population. To compare fairly, this map shows approvals per 1,000 residents — a supply adequacy rate. The national average is approximately 5.8 per 1,000. Melbourne leads at 8.56; Darwin trails at just 2.29. Hover over each state to see whether it sits above or below the national average.

Using the ABS standard of 2.5 persons per new dwelling, we can estimate how many approvals each city needs per 1,000 residents to house its growing population. Bars show actual approvals — blue when supply meets demand, red when it falls short. The orange tick mark shows the estimated need threshold. Darwin and Perth sit clearly left of their ticks, revealing the most acute supply shortfalls.

Population Growth vs Supply Rate

Each bubble is a state or territory; size represents population. Dashed lines mark the median population growth rate and median supply rate, dividing states into four supply-demand quadrants.

States in the top-right have both high growth and strong supply — Western Australia leads. States in the bottom-right are growing fast but not building fast enough.

Data joined from two ABS sources: Building Approvals 8731.0 (Mar 2026) and Regional Population 3218.0 (FY2024–25).

Section 5

The Supply–Demand Gap

The Housing Adequacy Score normalises supply against demand. A score of 1.0 means a city is exactly meeting its housing need; below 1.0 indicates undersupply. Darwin scores just 0.34 — building at barely a third of the rate needed to accommodate its growing population. Perth (0.84) and Brisbane (0.93) also fall short. Hobart scores above 4 due to near-zero population growth, not exceptional building activity.
