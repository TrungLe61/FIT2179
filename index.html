Australia's Housing Supply Crisis

Australia is building fewer homes than it needs. From 1983 to March 2026, building approvals have lurched through booms and busts driven by interest rate cycles, economic shocks, and policy changes — yet the national target of 240,000 new dwellings per year has rarely been met. This visualisation examines where homes are being approved, what types are being built, and whether supply is keeping pace with a rapidly growing population.

Section 1

The National Picture

Forty years of data reveal a market shaped by economic shocks and interest rate cycles. The GFC (2008) triggered a sharp fall in approvals; the mining boom briefly lifted them; COVID stimulus in 2020–21 produced a short-lived spike; and the 13 consecutive rate rises from May 2022 caused the steepest sustained decline in decades. Drag the navigator bar below the chart to zoom into any period.

Are We Building Enough?

The National Housing Accord requires approximately 240,000 new dwellings per year to keep pace with population growth. The rolling 12-month total has rarely reached this threshold, and currently sits well below it at around 160,000.

The 2021 stimulus boom briefly pushed approvals close to target, but 13 consecutive interest rate rises from May 2022 caused a steep fall — particularly in apartment approvals, which are highly sensitive to financing costs. At the current pace, Australia will fall approximately 80,000 dwellings short of the annual target.

Where Is Australia Building?

Building Activity by State

Select a state to explore its building footprint. The map zooms to that state and shows SA4 regions shaded by population growth (% per year), with the capital city plotted as a circle coloured by its approvals-per-1,000-residents rate.

Victoria leads nationally with 56,581 approvals, followed by New South Wales (49,515) and Queensland (38,229). Hover over a region or the city circle for full details.

The ACT's dramatic collapse (−37.8% YoY) stands out as the most severe decline, driven by the near-halt of apartment development in Canberra amid high interest rates.

While Victoria and NSW approve the most dwellings in raw terms, the per-capita picture tells a different story. Melbourne leads on rate (8.56 per 1,000), closely followed by Adelaide (7.94) and Perth (7.93). Darwin approves the fewest relative to its population, reflecting limited construction activity in the Territory.

Where Is Growth Accelerating?

Western Australia (+31.4%) is the standout performer, driven by strong migration inflows. The ACT (−37.8%) and South Australia (−5.2%) are the only jurisdictions recording declines. Blue = growth; orange = decline.

Three Views, One Geography

The same state boundaries reveal three different dimensions of the housing story: total approval volumes, per-capita supply rate, and year-on-year momentum — side by side. Victoria scores highest on volume; Melbourne leads on per-capita supply.

Section 3

What Type of Homes Are We Building?

The long-term shift from detached houses to apartments is one of the defining structural trends in Australian housing. In 1983, houses dominated; today apartments account for roughly 40% of all approvals nationally — and far more in Sydney and Melbourne. Drag the navigator bar to zoom into a specific period.

A Tale of Two Cities

Perth (79.6% houses) and Darwin (82.4%) are land-abundant cities where detached housing dominates. Canberra has the highest apartment share at 75.1% — reflecting deliberate densification policy.

Sydney sits at 61.3% apartments, driven by its constrained land market and high house prices pushing demand toward units. The housing mix matters for affordability: high apartment shares can indicate either a well-functioning density market or a failure to deliver affordable detached homes.

Section 4

Is Supply Meeting Demand?

Raw approval totals favour large states by population. To compare fairly, this map shows approvals per 1,000 residents — a supply adequacy rate. The national average is approximately 5.8 per 1,000. Melbourne leads at 8.56; Darwin trails at just 2.29. Hover over each state to see whether it sits above or below the national average.

Population growth is not evenly spread. The 20 fastest-growing local government areas are overwhelmingly concentrated in outer-suburban greenfield corridors — Melton and Wyndham in Victoria's west; Ipswich and Logan in south-east Queensland; Kwinana and Serpentine-Jarrahdale south of Perth. Circle size reflects the population growth rate.

Population Growth vs Supply Rate

Each bubble is a state or territory; size represents population. Dashed lines mark the approximate median population growth rate (1.8%) and national average supply rate (5.8 per 1,000), dividing the chart into four supply-demand quadrants.

States in the top-right have both high growth and strong supply — Western Australia leads. States in the bottom-right are growing fast but not building fast enough.

Data joined from two ABS sources: Building Approvals 8731.0 (Mar 2026) and Regional Population 3218.0 (FY2024–25).

Section 5

The Supply–Demand Gap

Each capital city's building supply rate (approvals per 1,000 residents, blue) is paired with its population demand pressure (annual growth %, red). Cities where the red dot sits far to the right of the blue dot face the most acute supply stress. Melbourne shows the best balance. Sydney has high demand but weak supply — only 5.95 approvals per 1,000 despite 1.35% annual population growth.
